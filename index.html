02 — Methodology

How We Read the Street

This study uses qualitative research methods to interpret a hand-drawn neighborhood map. Each element on the original map — a dot, a sticky note, a photograph — carries meaning. Together, they form a rich spatial narrative about senior life in Marpole.

The Color Coding System

Four dot colors were applied to locations throughout the map. Each color represents a different dimension of the research: place significance, social activity, emotional warmth, or exclusion.

Places

Named locations, businesses, community nodes. Physical anchors of neighborhood life.

Senior Gathering

Sites where seniors were observed congregating — spontaneous or routine social activity.

Warmth

Spaces with a felt sense of welcome, social ease, and belonging. Good vibes.

Cold / Unwelcoming

Areas perceived as socially excluding, uncomfortable, or inaccessible to seniors.

Qualitative Methods Used

Spatial Analysis

Plotting dots on a street-level map to identify geographic clusters, gaps, and patterns in how seniors inhabit the neighborhood.

Thematic Coding

Sticky notes were analyzed for recurring themes: social routines, named activities, barriers to participation, and intergenerational moments.

Behavior Mapping

Documenting observable behaviors — sitting, talking, walking, playing — to understand how space is actually used versus how it was designed.

Emotional Geography

Using participant-drawn distinctions between warm and cold spaces to map the affective dimension of place: how environments feel, not just function.

04 — Key Findings

What the Map Reveals

01
Spatial Pattern

Senior activity clusters around walkable, low-stakes nodes

The highest concentrations of yellow dots appear within 200m of the Marpole Community Centre and along Granville Street — both walkable, flat, and rich with pause opportunities. Seniors gravitate toward routes where sitting, stopping, and talking are possible at low cost.

02
Emotional Geography

Warmth correlates with visibility and social legibility

Pink dots consistently appear where seniors can see and be seen — café windows, open forecourts, pharmacies with seating. Warmth is not a design feature; it is the felt consequence of being acknowledged. Spaces where staff recognize faces generate the most pink.

03
Intergenerational Contact

Cross-generational spaces emerge where seniors are already welcome

Every photo placement on the original map appears within or adjacent to a pink dot. Intergenerational interaction doesn't create warmth — it flourishes in spaces that are already warm. Design for seniors first; inclusive energy follows.

04
Exclusion by Design

Cold spaces are defined by absence, not hostility

Blue dots mark not dangerous or unfriendly places, but empty ones. No benches, no shelter, no reason to pause. Marine Drive's northern edge scores cold not because it rejects seniors — but because it was never designed for them at all. Exclusion through omission.

05
Behavioral Insight

Named relationships are the warmest infrastructure of all

The most frequently pink-dotted spaces share one trait: seniors are known by name. Whether by a café barista, a pharmacist, or a program coordinator — named recognition transforms a building into a place. The study's sticky notes reveal that seniors navigate Marpole through a personal geography of "people who know me," rather than services or architecture.

"The most socially alive corners of Marpole were never built to be gathering places. They became ones because someone remembered a name."
05 — Activities & Behaviors

What Seniors Actually Do

The sticky notes on the original map describe specific behaviors — not abstract demographics. Senior social life in Marpole is characterized by low-energy, stationary, and relationship-driven activity. These behaviors are the heartbeat of neighborhood warmth.

☕
Café Sitting

Extended stays at window seats. Watching the street. Being seen. "My Tuesday place."

🐕
Dog Walking

Daily routine creating predictable, repeated social contact. "Dana the dog walker" — recognized by name.

🛒
Market Browsing

Slow, purposeful shopping at the Chinese market. Meeting ground for Cantonese-speaking seniors.

💃
Social Dancing

Evening dance events at the community centre. Observed as the highest-energy social gathering in the study.

🪑
Bench Sitting

Spontaneous gathering at benches, bus stops, and stoops. Socializing without a program or schedule.

🧘
Drop-in Programs

Fitness, crafts, and wellness at the community centre. Social anchor for seniors without strong local networks.

👁
Watching Kids Play

Lingering near school grounds during recess. Spontaneous intergenerational warmth — no program required.

💊
Pharmacy Visits

Transformed by familiar staff into social rituals. Waiting becomes talking. Prescriptions become connections.

🚌
Transit Waiting

Bus shelters become social infrastructure. Same people, same times. Strangers become regulars over weeks.

06 — Reflection & Implications

Designing for Belonging

This study reveals that senior-friendly urban space is less about specialized programming and more about the cumulative effect of small social cues. The implications reach beyond Marpole — they speak to how any city can foster belonging for aging populations.

🏙

For Urban Design

Benches, shelter, and flat routes are not amenities — they are access. Cold spaces in Marpole are cold because the physical vocabulary of rest was never installed. Every missing bench is a small act of exclusion.

🤝

For Social Infrastructure

Staff recognition and named relationships are the most cost-effective warmth intervention available. Training service workers to remember names and routines may do more for senior wellbeing than any architectural renovation.

🗺

For Emotional Geography

How a space feels shapes whether it is used. Fear of feeling rushed, invisible, or out of place is a genuine barrier to mobility. Affective mapping — listening to how spaces feel — should precede any redesign process.

🌱

For Intergenerational Planning

Designing for cross-generational connection requires first designing for seniors. When seniors feel welcome, younger populations follow into the same spaces. The relationship flows one way first.

🔍

For Research Methods

Qualitative spatial methods — dots, notes, photographs, hand-drawn maps — reveal textures of lived experience that surveys cannot. Emotional geography is a legitimate and necessary lens for equitable urban planning.

💡

For Immediate Action

The changes that would most transform senior life in Marpole are inexpensive: more benches, shorter crossing times, bilingual staff at community touchpoints, and spaces that allow lingering without the pressure to consume or perform.

"A city is only as warm as the welcome it extends to its most vulnerable members. In Marpole, that welcome exists — but unevenly, held in the hands of a few named staff and a handful of patient benches."
